🔰 What Are Fundamental Rights? — Origins & Philosophy

Fundamental Rights emerged as a direct response to colonial oppression. The British colonial regime arbitrarily arrested people (Rowlatt Act, 1919), censored press, and denied basic freedoms without legal recourse. The Constituent Assembly — having lived through that — deliberately created a chapter of enforceable rights that even the legislature could not take away.

The philosophical roots are dual: natural rights theory (rights inherent to human dignity, not granted by the State) + social contract theory (the citizen surrenders some freedom to the State in exchange for protection of fundamental liberties). Part III reflects both.

Key Structural Features of FRs:
  • Justiciable: Enforceable in courts — unlike DPSPs (non-justiciable) or Fundamental Duties (not enforceable per se)
  • Not absolute: Every FR has grounds on which reasonable restrictions can be imposed — FRs are not a "blank cheque"
  • Primarily vertical: Operate against the State (Art.12). But Art.17 (untouchability) and Art.23 (forced labour) have horizontal effect — they operate against private persons too
  • Non-waivable: You cannot voluntarily give up a FR — held in Behram Khurshid Pesikaka v. State of Bombay (1955)
  • Amendable but basic structure protected: Parliament can abridge FRs (Art.368) but cannot destroy their "core" — Kesavananda Bharati (1973)
Negative vs Positive Obligations: Originally, FRs were purely negative — they said "the State shall NOT." But through judicial expansion of Art.21, some FRs now impose positive obligations: the State must provide legal aid (Art.39A via Art.21), ensure education (Art.21A), protect health, etc. This shift from negative to positive rights is a major Mains theme.

Source inspiration: Art.14 (Equal Protection) ← US 14th Amendment | Art.14 (Equality before Law) ← English Common Law | Art.19 freedoms ← US Bill of Rights | Art.21 (Life & Liberty) ← US 5th/14th Amendments | Art.32 (Writs) ← English Writ Jurisdiction

⚙️ Article 12 — Defining "The State" (The Gateway Article)

Art.12 is the gateway — without understanding it, you cannot know who your FRs protect you against. It defines "State" for purposes of Part III:

  1. The Government and Parliament of India
  2. The Government and Legislature of each State
  3. All local or other authorities within the territory of India
  4. All local or other authorities under the control of the Government of India
"Other Authorities" — The Evolving Test (Critical for UPSC)
The SC has progressively widened "other authorities" through a series of cases:
  • University of Madras v. Shantha Bai (1954): Ejusdem generis rule — "other authorities" means only government-like bodies. This narrow view was later rejected.
  • Electricity Board, Rajasthan v. Mohan Lal (1967): Any body created by statute for public purposes = "State." This widened the definition.
  • Ramana Dayaram Shetty v. IAAI (1979): The SC laid down a multi-factor test — (i) entire share capital held by government, (ii) enjoys monopoly status conferred by State, (iii) performs public functions, (iv) deep and pervasive State control. Not all factors need to be present.
  • Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib (1981): Applied the Shetty test to Regional Engineering Colleges — held to be "State" since they were government-funded and government-controlled.
  • Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002): The test is whether the body is "financially, functionally, and administratively dominated by or under the control of the Government." CSIR laboratories = State.
  • Zee Telefilms v. Union of India (2005): BCCI is NOT a State because it is not created by statute, the government doesn't control it, and cricket is not a monopoly granted by the State.
Judiciary & Art.12: Courts are NOT "State" when exercising their adjudicatory functions — judicial decisions are not subject to FR challenge. But when courts make Rules (like Rules of Court), they act as a "State" and those rules can be challenged.

⚙️ Article 13 — The Enforcement Weapon + Three Key Doctrines

Art.13 makes FRs judicially enforceable. It does two things: (1) declares all pre-constitutional laws inconsistent with FRs to be void to the extent of inconsistency [Art.13(1)], and (2) says the State shall not make any law that abridges FRs, and any such law is void to the extent of contravention [Art.13(2)].

Art.13(3) defines "law" broadly to include ordinances, orders, bye-laws, rules, regulations, notifications, customs, and usages having force of law. So even a government notification can be challenged as violating FRs.

Critical: Does Art.13 apply to Constitutional Amendments?
The SC initially said YES in Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967) — Parliament cannot amend FRs at all. But the 24th Amendment (1971) overturned this by expressly stating Art.368 empowers Parliament to amend any part of the Constitution including FRs. Then Kesavananda Bharati (1973) gave the final answer: Parliament CAN amend FRs but CANNOT destroy their "basic structure." Art.13 applies to ordinary legislation — not to constitutional amendments that respect basic structure.
Doctrine 1 — Severability (Doctrine of Blue Pencil): If a law has some provisions that violate FRs and some that don't, the court only strikes down the offending part — if separable. The test: would the legislature have enacted the valid part alone, even without the invalid part? AK Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950) is an early example. The court severs only what's unconstitutional.
Doctrine 2 — Eclipse: Pre-1950 laws inconsistent with FRs become dormant, not void. They are "eclipsed" by the FR like a solar eclipse — the sun (FR) blocks the law's operation but doesn't destroy it. If the FR is later amended (widened for State restriction), the law comes back to life. Note: This doctrine applies only to pre-constitutional laws. For non-citizens, such laws remain fully operative even in their eclipsed state. Key case: Bhikaji Narain Dhakras v. State of MP (1955).
Doctrine 3 — Waiver (Non-Waiver): FRs are conferred not just for individual benefit but as matters of constitutional policy — they cannot be waived. If you sign a contract saying "I waive my FR to equality," that clause is void. Behram Khurshid Pesikaka (1955): Even personal benefit cannot justify waiver.
Doctrine 4 (Bonus) — Colourable Legislation: "What cannot be done directly cannot be done indirectly." If a legislature lacks competence to pass a law directly, it cannot achieve the same result by disguising the law. Tested under Art.13 when the substance (not form) of a law violates FRs.

⚖️ Article 14 — Right to Equality: Two Limbs + Four Doctrines

Art.14 has two distinct limbs, though both flow from equality:

LimbWhat it meansOrigin
Equality before LawNo person is above the law. The law applies the same way to everyone — the PM, a judge, or a street vendor can all be prosecuted. Negative concept — the State shall not give special privileges.English Common Law (Dicey's Rule of Law)
Equal Protection of LawsLike must be treated alike. The law must operate equally on all persons in similar circumstances. Positive concept — the State must give equal treatment to equals.US 14th Amendment
The Reasonable Classification Exception (The Most Tested Point):
Art.14 does NOT mean all laws must apply identically to everyone — that would be absurd (you can't treat adults and children identically). The State can classify people for differential treatment, provided:
(i) The classification is based on an intelligible differentia — there must be a real, discernible distinction (e.g., by income, by occupation, by age).
(ii) The differentia has a rational nexus with the object of the law — the classification must relate logically to the purpose the law seeks to achieve.
Both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously. State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar (1952) first articulated this two-prong test.
The Anti-Arbitrariness Doctrine (New Dimension): In EP Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (1974), Justice Bhagwati gave Art.14 a new dimension: "Equality is antithetic to arbitrariness." Any arbitrary, capricious state action — even without discrimination — violates Art.14. This was extended in Maneka Gandhi (1978): procedure that is arbitrary is void even if "established by law." This doctrine makes Art.14 much wider than the reasonable classification test alone.
Exceptions to Art.14 — Where Inequality is Permitted:
  • Foreign Sovereigns & Diplomats: Enjoy immunity from suit in Indian courts (Vienna Convention) — not a violation of Art.14 because it's based on international law obligations.
  • President and Governors: Art.361 gives them immunity from civil and criminal proceedings during their term. SC in Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006) clarified this doesn't mean absolute immunity — their official acts can be examined.
  • UN and its officials: Immunity under the UN (Privileges and Immunities) Act.
Art.14 and Legislation — Key Rules:
  • A law cannot be challenged on Art.14 grounds merely because it has a harsh effect on some — as long as classification is reasonable.
  • Art.14 applies to the State's actions, not to private acts. But Art.12's "State" definition is broad enough to catch many quasi-governmental bodies.
  • A taxation law can differentiate between income groups — reasonable classification.
  • A law giving different rates to different industries — valid if the differentia is rational.

⚖️ Article 15 — Prohibition of Discrimination: All Clauses Decoded

Art.15(1): The State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Note: "place of birth" ≠ "domicile" or "residence." Discrimination based on current residence may be allowed.

Art.15(2): No citizen shall be subject to disability, liability, restriction regarding (i) access to shops, public restaurants, hotels; (ii) use of wells, tanks, bathing ghats, roads, or places of public resort maintained wholly or partly out of State funds or dedicated to the use of general public — on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. This extends the prohibition to private persons too for these specific contexts.

Critical "only" — The Word That Changes Everything: Art.15(1) says "on grounds only of" religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. This means if a classification is based on these grounds PLUS something else (e.g., income + caste combined), it may be saved. The moment there are multiple grounds, the "only" qualifier is not met, and the discrimination may be permissible.
Art.15 vs Art.16: Art.15 covers general discrimination in all fields. Art.16 specifically covers equality of opportunity in public employment. If the issue is a job/government post, Art.16 is the specific article; Art.15 is the general one. UPSC frequently tests this distinction.

⚖️ Article 16 — Equality in Public Employment: Reservation Law Simplified

Art.16(1): Equality of opportunity for all citizens in matters relating to employment or appointment to any office under the State. Art.16(2): No citizen shall be discriminated against in public employment on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, descent, place of birth, or residence. The Indra Sawhney Case (Mandal Case) — Most Important Reservation Judgment:
  • 50% ceiling on total reservations — judge-made rule, NOT in Constitution
  • Creamy layer must be excluded from OBC reservations
  • No reservation in promotions for OBCs (only Art.16(4) covers initial appointments for them)
  • Reservation is an exception to merit, not a right in itself
  • A "backward class" need not be a caste, but caste can be one indicator of backwardness
  • Periodical review of the backward class list is mandatory
Art.335 Linkage: Art.335 says claims of SCs/STs to services must be taken into consideration "consistently with the maintenance of efficiency of administration." However, the 82nd Amendment (2000) added a proviso: nothing in Art.335 prevents the State from making provision for relaxation of qualifying marks or lowering the evaluation standards for SCs/STs in promotions. So efficiency cannot be an absolute bar to SC/ST reservation in promotions.
Recent: EWS Reservation (103rd Amendment, 2019): EWS reservation is 10% over and above the existing 50% (so total can reach 60% for that year). Criteria: annual family income below ₹8 lakh AND agricultural land less than 5 acres AND residential flat below 1000 sq ft AND residential plot below 100 sq yards in notified municipality. SCs/STs/OBCs are EXCLUDED from EWS because they already have their own reservations. SC upheld this by 3:2 in Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (2022).

⚖️ Article 17 — Untouchability Abolished | Article 18 — No Titles

Art.17 is uniquely worded: "Untouchability is abolished and its practice in any form is forbidden." It uses the word "abolished" — not "the State shall not." This means it operates against private persons too — one of the rare instances of horizontal application of FRs.

  • Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955 (originally Untouchability Offences Act, 1955): Makes practice of untouchability a punishable offence. Imprisonment up to 6 months and/or fine.
  • SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989: Goes further — punishes specific atrocities against SC/ST members including social and economic boycott, humiliation, sexual offences. SC has held in Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra (2018) that anticipatory bail can be granted in false cases, but then reversed this in 2019 (larger bench reinstated original stringent provisions).
  • "Untouchability" is not defined in the Constitution — but it refers to the practice of treating certain castes as untouchable based on birth.

Art.18 — No person shall confer any title (other than military or academic distinction) and no citizen shall accept any title from any foreign State.

Bharat Ratna / Padma Awards — NOT Titles under Art.18: The SC confirmed in Balaji Raghavan v. Union of India (1996) that the National Honour Awards (Bharat Ratna, Padma Vibhushan, Padma Bhushan, Padma Shri) are NOT "titles" for the purpose of Art.18(1). They cannot be used as prefixes or suffixes to names. They are merely a form of State recognition.
What IS banned under Art.18: Feudal titles like "Sir," "Rai Bahadur," "Khan Bahadur," "Diwan Bahadur" — all colonial-era honorifics that conferred social superiority. The article aims at a classless, egalitarian society. Military distinctions like "General," "Colonel" or academic ones like "Dr.," "Professor" are explicitly allowed.

🗣️ Article 19 — Six Freedoms: Each Freedom + Its Restrictions

Art.19(1) guarantees six freedoms to citizens only. These are NOT absolute — Art.19(2)–(6) allow the State to impose reasonable restrictions through law (not executive order alone). The word "reasonable" is judicially reviewed — a restriction upheld only if proportionate to the evil it seeks to prevent.

  • Right to carry on a criminal business or profession (e.g., running a brothel)

  • Right of press to publish (press freedom flows from 19(1)(a)) — Indian Express v. UOI (1985)
  • Right to internet access (internet shutdown cases — Anuradha Bhasin 2020)
  • Right to silence (not speaking) — right not to be compelled to speak
  • Right to know about candidates' antecedents before elections — ADR case (2002)
  • Right to hoist the national flag — Union of India v. Naveen Jindal (2004)
"Reasonable Restriction" Test — How Courts Assess: The SC applies a proportionality test: (1) Is there a legitimate aim? (2) Is the measure rationally connected to the aim? (3) Is the measure the least restrictive option? (4) Is the balance between the restriction and the right struck correctly? This imported from European human rights law into Indian jurisprudence through K.S. Puttaswamy (2017).

🛡️ Article 20 — Three Protections Against Arbitrary Conviction (Cannot be Suspended)

Art.20 gives three protections to persons accused of offences. It is available to ALL PERSONS (not just citizens) and CANNOT be suspended even during National Emergency (44th Amendment guarantee). Each clause has precise scope.

ClauseProtectionScope + Nuances
Art.20(1)
Ex-Post Facto		 No conviction for act not an offence at the time of commission. No penalty greater than that prescribed at the time. What is protected: Criminal conviction only — not civil liability. A tax law cannot be applied retrospectively in criminal prosecutions.
What is NOT protected: Procedural laws can be changed and applied retroactively (only substantive law defining the offence or penalty is protected). Beneficial laws (reducing penalty) can apply retrospectively.
Preventive detention: Art.20(1) does not apply to preventive detention (no conviction involved).
Art.20(2)
Double Jeopardy		 "No person shall be prosecuted and punished for the same offence more than once." Both conditions needed: Must be prosecuted AND punished in the first instance. If only prosecuted but acquitted, a fresh prosecution by a different court for a different offence arising from same facts is possible.
Departmental proceedings: Art.20(2) applies to judicial prosecution, NOT to departmental enquiries (an employee can face departmental action AND criminal prosecution for the same act).
Comparison with CrPC S.300: Sec.300 of CrPC gives wider protection — bars second trial even if acquitted. Art.20(2) bars only if previously convicted AND punished.
Art.20(3)
Self-Incrimination		 No person accused of an offence shall be compelled to be a witness against himself. Three conditions: (i) Must be an "accused person" (ii) Compulsion must be used (iii) Must be "witness against himself" — i.e., testimonial compulsion, not physical evidence.
Testimonial vs Physical: Providing fingerprints, blood samples, hair samples, breath analysis — NOT protected by Art.20(3) (physical evidence). Forced statement, narco-analysis, lie detector — ARE protected (testimonial). Selvi v. State of Karnataka (2010): Involuntary narco/lie-detector tests violate Art.20(3) and Art.21.
Only for criminal proceedings: Does NOT apply to civil proceedings. Does NOT apply before enquiry commissions.
Pre-arrest: Art.20(3) applies only after formal accusation — a witness (not yet accused) has no protection under Art.20(3). But Art.21 protects even witnesses from unfair treatment.

❤️ Article 21 — Right to Life: The Most Expansive Fundamental Right

"No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law." — Eight words ("procedure established by law") have generated India's most extensive body of constitutional jurisprudence.

The Evolution of Art.21 — Three Phases:
Phase 1 — AK Gopalan (1950): The SC interpreted Art.21 narrowly. "Procedure established by law" means any procedure — even an unjust one — as long as it exists in some law. Rights under Arts.19, 21, 22 were read as separate compartments. A law passing Art.22's procedural requirements could deprive life even if it violated Art.19.

Phase 2 — Maneka Gandhi (1978): Watershed. SC overruled Gopalan. Held that (i) Articles 14, 19, 21 are not separate compartments — a law depriving life must satisfy all three; (ii) "Procedure established by law" must be just, fair, and reasonable — importing the substance of due process into Art.21; (iii) Right to live with dignity, not just physical existence, is protected.

Phase 3 — Puttaswamy (2017): 9-judge bench unanimously recognised Right to Privacy as a FR under Art.21. The Court used a proportionality standard to test government intrusions. This phase sees Art.21 reach into data protection, informational autonomy, sexual autonomy, and identity rights.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) — Why It Matters: Mrs. Maneka Gandhi's passport was impounded without giving any reasons. The SC held: (1) "Personal liberty" in Art.21 has the widest amplitude — it includes all varieties of rights that go to make up the personal liberties of persons; (2) the procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable; (3) Art.14's anti-arbitrariness doctrine now flows into Art.21; (4) you cannot impound a passport without hearing the person — principles of natural justice are part of Art.21.
Right to Die — Important nuance:
  • P. Rathinam v. Union of India (1994): 2-judge bench held attempt to suicide (Sec.309 IPC) unconstitutional — right to die included in Art.21.

  • Common Cause v. Union of India (2018): SC recognised passive euthanasia (withdrawing life support from terminally ill patient) as permissible. Permitted "living wills" / advance directives.
  • Sec.309 IPC (attempt to suicide) was DECRIMINALISED by Mental Healthcare Act, 2017 — but note: decriminalised ≠ constitutionally void.

📚 Article 21A — Right to Education (The 86th Amendment, 2002)

"The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State may, by law, determine."

  • Implemented through the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education (RTE) Act, 2009, which came into force on April 1, 2010.
  • Key provisions of RTE Act: 25% seats reserved in private unaided schools for children from disadvantaged groups and weaker sections; no detention policy up to Class 8 (partially reversed — now schools can hold back students in Grades 5 and 8 after failed exams); no capitation fee; age-appropriate admission.
  • The 86th Amendment also amended Art.45 (DPSP) — changed from "State shall endeavour to provide free and compulsory education" to age 6–14 to "State shall endeavour to provide early childhood care and education for children below age 6."
  • Art.51A was amended to add a Fundamental Duty: "a parent or guardian to provide opportunities for education to his child or ward between the age of 6 and 14 years."
RTE Act — Not Applicable to: Minority educational institutions under Art.30(1) — this was contested. In Society for Un-aided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012), SC upheld 25% reservation requirement for unaided non-minority private schools. But in Pramati Educational and Cultural Trust v. Union of India (2014), SC held RTE Act does NOT apply to unaided minority schools — they're protected by Art.30.
Critical gap in Art.21A: It covers only 6–14 years. Children below 6 (covered by Art.45 DPSP — early childhood care) and above 14 (higher education) are NOT covered as a FR. The demand to extend RTE to 0–18 years remains pending.

🔒 Article 22 — Safeguards Against Arbitrary Detention: Two Regimes

Art.22 covers two distinct situations with separate safeguards — ordinary arrests AND preventive detention. These are completely different legal regimes and UPSC tests this distinction.

3 months without Advisory Board approval (judges or ex-judges) ② Grounds to be communicated ASAP (but if against public interest, grounds need not be disclosed) ③ Right to make representation against detention NO right to consult lawyer; NO right before Magistrate NOT available to enemy aliens Key laws: NSA 1980, COFEPOSA 1974, UAPA 1967, MPNSA (state level). Parliament can extend beyond 3 months.

Art.22 — Ordinary Arrest safeguards vs Preventive Detention safeguards

Preventive Detention — UPSC Critical Points:
  • Purpose: Detain someone to prevent them from doing something harmful — no trial, no conviction needed
  • Parliament can authorise detention beyond 3 months if Advisory Board confirms it
  • Parliament can prescribe under what circumstances, and for what class of cases, a person may be detained for more than 3 months (Art.22(7))
  • Grounds need NOT be disclosed if the authority considers it against public interest — this is the controversial part
  • ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976) — the infamous "Habeas Corpus case": SC held that during Emergency, Art.21 can be suspended, so even Habeas Corpus against illegal detention is not available. This judgment (by 4:1 — only Justice Khanna dissented) is widely regarded as the SC's darkest hour. It was effectively overruled by the 44th Amendment (1978) which protected Art.20 & 21 even during Emergency.

⛓️ Article 23 — Against Exploitation | Article 24 — Child Labour

Art.23 prohibits (i) traffic in human beings, (ii) begar, and (iii) other similar forms of forced labour. Any contravention is a criminal offence punishable by law.

  • Traffic in human beings: Covers slavery, devadasi system, flesh trade, and all forms of human trafficking. The SC in PUCL v. Union of India (2011) directed comprehensive measures against trafficking.
  • Begar: Forced labour without payment — traditionally extracted from poor/lower-caste people by landlords. Now criminalised.
  • "Other similar forms": SC in People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (PUDR case, 1982) — payment of wages below minimum wage = forced labour violating Art.23. The State has an obligation to enforce minimum wage laws.
  • Exception: Art.23 does NOT prevent the State from imposing compulsory service for public purposes (e.g., military conscription, jury service) — but such service cannot discriminate on grounds of religion, race, caste, or class.
  • Horizontal effect: Art.23 applies against private persons — a private employer forcing bonded labour violates Art.23, not just Art.21.
  • Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act, 1976: Enforces Art.23. The SC in Neerja Chaudhary v. State of MP (1984) directed state governments to identify, release, and rehabilitate bonded labourers.

Art.24 — No child below 14 years shall be employed in any factory or mine or engaged in any other hazardous employment.

  • Original Art.24 restricted only factories, mines, and hazardous employment. It did not ban all child labour.
  • Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act, 2016: Extended the prohibition to all forms of employment for children below 14 (not just hazardous). Children aged 14–18 cannot work in hazardous occupations. Exception: family enterprises and entertainment industry with conditions (no night work, no work during school hours).
  • The SC in MC Mehta v. State of Tamil Nadu (Sivakasi match factory case, 1996) directed minimum wage and rehabilitation fund for child workers — linking Art.24 with Art.21 (right to live with dignity) and DPSPs.

🕌 Articles 25–28 — Freedom of Religion: Four Articles, Core Tests

Art.25 — Freedom of conscience, and the right to freely profess, practice, and propagate religion. Subject to: public order, morality, health, and other FRs.

Essential Religious Practices Test (ERP Test): Art.25 protects only practices "essential" to the religion. A practice that is not essential (even if traditional) is not protected. The Court decides what is essential — this has been controversial. Examples:
  • Triple talaq (instant talaq) — SC in Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017) held it unconstitutional (3:2). It was NOT an essential religious practice of Islam. Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019 criminalised it.
  • Sabarimala — The temple's practice of excluding women aged 10–50 was not an "essential religious practice" of the Ayyappa faith — Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (2018), 4:1 majority. Referred to larger bench in 2019 for reconsideration along with similar issues.
  • Ananda Marga monks' practice of tandava dance in public — held NOT essential — Acharya Jagdishwarananda Avadhuta v. Commissioner of Police (1984)
  • Wearing kirpan for Sikhs — held an essential practice (Art.25(1) explicitly permits Sikhs to carry kirpans as religious requirement)
Art.25(2) — State's Power to Regulate:
  • State can regulate or restrict any economic, financial, political, or other secular activity associated with religious practice.
  • State can provide for social welfare and reform and throw open Hindu religious institutions of a public character to all classes of Hindus (including SCs).
  • This was used to justify the Hindu temple entry laws — the SC upheld laws requiring temples to allow Dalit entry as they deal with secular (public access) aspects.

Art.26 — Every religious denomination has rights to: (a) establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes; (b) manage its own affairs in matters of religion; (c) own and acquire movable and immovable property; (d) administer such property in accordance with law.

Art.25 vs Art.26:
  • Art.25 = individual right of conscience and religious freedom
  • Art.26 = collective right of a religious denomination to manage its own affairs
  • "Religious denomination" = a body of people with common faith, organisation, and designation — e.g., Ramakrishna Math, Aurobindo Ashram, specific sects within larger religions
  • The State can regulate secular/administrative aspects even of denominational institutions (like keeping accounts) — but cannot interfere in purely religious matters (like who can be a priest)
  • Shirur Mutt case (1954): Distinction between "religious matters" (not regulated) and "secular matters associated with religion" (can be regulated) was drawn first here

Art.27 — No person shall be compelled to pay any taxes, the proceeds of which are specifically appropriated for the payment of expenses for the promotion or maintenance of any particular religion or religious denomination.

  • A fee (not tax) for temple administration purposes is permissible.
  • Tax collected for Haj subsidy — contested; SC in 2012 asked the government to phase it out as it violates the secular principle (not Art.27 per se).
  • Entry fees to religious places are fees, not taxes — permissible.

Art.28 — Three tiers regarding religious instruction in educational institutions:

🏫 Articles 29–30 — Cultural & Educational Rights: Minority Rights in Detail

Art.29(1) — Any section of citizens residing in any part of India having a distinct language, script, or culture of its own shall have the right to conserve the same. Available to ALL sections of citizens — majority and minority alike (unlike Art.30 which is only for minorities).

Art.29(2) — No citizen shall be denied admission into any educational institution maintained by the State or receiving aid out of State funds on grounds only of religion, race, caste, language, or any of them.

Art.29(2) vs Art.15: Art.29(2) specifically covers admission to educational institutions. Art.15(1) covers general discrimination. A minority institution claiming Art.30 rights can still be subject to Art.29(2) — it cannot exclude students only on grounds of religion/caste if it receives State aid.

Art.30(1) — All minorities (religious or linguistic) shall have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice. Art.30(1A) (added by 44th Amendment) — In fixing compensation for compulsory acquisition of minority institution's property, the adequacy of such compensation shall not be questioned on ground of Art.31.

Art.30(2) — State shall not, in granting aid to educational institutions, discriminate against any educational institution on the ground that it is under the management of a minority.

TMA Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002) — 11-Judge Bench — Landmark:
  • Who is a "minority"? Determined on a State-wise basis (not national basis) — so Hindus can be a minority in Meghalaya, Mizoram, etc.
  • Minority institutions can have their own admission process for minority students.
  • But for non-minority seats (in aided institutions), normal merit-based admission applies.
  • State can regulate academic standards, fee structure (to prevent profiteering), and infrastructure — but cannot completely take over administration.
  • Right to administer does NOT mean right to maladminister — efficiency of administration can be regulated.
PA Inamdar v. State of Maharashtra (2005) — Extends TMA Pai: Unaided minority institutions have full freedom to admit their own students. The 93rd Amendment (Art.15(5)) that enabled reservations in private unaided institutions does NOT apply to minority institutions. This was the conflict between Art.15(5) and Art.30.
"Linguistic Minority" — Two Important Points:
(1) Every State can have its own linguistic minorities — Tamil speakers in Maharashtra, for instance, can claim Art.30 rights.
(2) There is no definition of "minority" in the Constitution. The National Commission for Minority Educational Institutions (NCMEI) Act, 2004 provides a statutory mechanism for granting minority status to educational institutions.

❤️ Article 32 — Constitutional Remedies: The Heart & Soul + All Five Writs

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar in the Constituent Assembly: "If I was asked to name any particular article in this Constitution as the most important — an article without which this Constitution would be a nullity — I could not refer to any other article except this one. It is the very soul of the Constitution and the very heart of it."

Art.32 guarantees the right to move the SC for enforcement of FRs — this RIGHT ITSELF IS A FUNDAMENTAL RIGHT. Unlike Art.226 (HC), Art.32 cannot be denied to any person whose FR has been violated.

Q1. 'Right to Privacy' is protected under which Article of the Constitution of India?

B. Article 19
C. Article 21

✓ Correct Answer: C — Article 21

⚠️ UPSC Trap Analysis

Elimination: Art.15 = discrimination | Art.19 = specific enumerated freedoms | Art.29 = minorities' cultural rights. B. Legal right available to any person
C. Fundamental Right available to citizens only

✓ Correct Answer: B — Legal right available to any person

2021 2 Marks LOOKALIKE TERM Prelims

Q4. A legislation which confers on the executive or administrative authority an unguided and uncontrolled discretionary power in the matter of application of law violates which one of the following Articles?

A. Article 14
B. Article 28
C. Article 32
D. Article 44
✓ Correct Answer: A — Article 14

Art.14 prohibits arbitrary state action. The SC has evolved the principle that excessive delegation of unguided discretion is inherently arbitrary and violates Art.14. This is the "anti-arbitrariness" doctrine. A law giving bureaucrats unchecked power = arbitrary = unconstitutional.

⚠️ UPSC Trap Analysis

The Trap: Students confuse this with Art.32 (remedy for FR violation) or Art.44 (DPSP for uniform civil code — which is a common distractor). Art.28 is about religious instruction in schools — completely unrelated.

Recall cue: Arbitrariness = Art.14. This was established in EP Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (1974) and extended in Maneka Gandhi (1978).

2023 2 Marks STATEMENT COMBO Prelims

Q6. Consider the following statements:

B. Both are correct but II does not explain I

Q. "The expanding horizons of Article 21 reflect the Supreme Court's commitment to substantive constitutionalism." Critically examine. [15 Marks, ~250 words]

Introduction

Article 21's journey from a narrow procedural guarantee to a substantive charter of human rights represents the most significant judicial achievement in Indian constitutional history, anchored in the Supreme Court's evolution from AK Gopalan (1950) to Puttaswamy (2017).

Expansion: From Procedure to Substance

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) was the watershed moment. The Court overruled AK Gopalan and held that "procedure established by law" must be just, fair, and reasonable — importing American due process into Art.21. This made Art.21 a living document capable of absorbing new rights.

Subsequently, the Court read into Art.21: right to livelihood (Olga Tellis, 1985), right to education (leading to Art.21A), right to health and medical aid (Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity, 1996), right to speedy trial (Hussainara Khatoon, 1979), right to clean environment (Subhash Kumar, 1991), right to dignity of prisoners, and most recently, right to privacy (Puttaswamy, 2017).

Critical Assessment

Positives: Judicial activism through Art.21 has filled legislative gaps, providing relief to marginalised groups when Parliament remained inactive. It has made the right to life genuinely meaningful and not merely formal.

Concerns: Unguided expansion risks making Art.21 a "super-right" that eclipses legislative space. Some scholars argue the court is engaging in social engineering beyond its mandate. Further, enforcement of positive socio-economic rights through Art.21 strains institutional capacity.

Conclusion

Art.21's expansion embodies substantive constitutionalism — the idea that rights must have real-world meaning. The challenge ahead is to balance judicial creativity with separation of powers, ensuring Art.21 remains an instrument of justice rather than litigation-overload.

2019 10 Marks Mains GS-II

Q. The Directive Principles of State Policy which are fundamental in the governance of the country cannot be isolated from the Fundamental Rights guaranteed to the people. Examine. [10 Marks, ~150 words]

Introduction

The Constitution's framers deliberately placed FRs and DPSPs in a complementary relationship — the former protecting individuals from state overreach, the latter guiding the state toward a just social order.

Interconnection

From conflict to harmony: Early jurisprudence (State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan, 1951) held FRs supreme over DPSPs. However, after multiple amendments and the Basic Structure doctrine, the SC evolved the harmonious construction approach in Minerva Mills (1980): neither FRs nor DPSPs can completely override each other.

Art.31C (42nd Amendment) immunised laws giving effect to DPSPs from challenge under Art.14 & Art.19 — though the SC in Minerva Mills struck down the part that removed judicial review.

Practical fusion: Right to education (Art.21A) implements DPSP Art.45. Right to livelihood under Art.21 mirrors DPSP Art.39(a). Art.47 (public health DPSP) informed the right to health under Art.21.

Conclusion

FRs are the means; DPSPs are the end. Together, they constitute the constitutional vision of India as a welfare state where individual liberty and social justice coexist. Treating them as rivals, rather than partners, misreads the Constitution's transformative intent.

MCQ 01 — Direct Recall
ABSOLUTE WORD

Which Fundamental Right cannot be suspended even during a National Emergency under Article 352?

A. Article 19 (Freedom of Speech)
B. Article 21 (Right to Life)
C. Article 32 (Right to Constitutional Remedies)
D. Article 14 (Right to Equality)
✓ Correct Answer: B — Article 21

Under Art.359, the President can suspend enforcement of FRs during National Emergency — EXCEPT Art.20 (protection from conviction) and Art.21 (right to life). These two remain operative always. Art.19 is automatically suspended during Art.352 Emergency. Art.32 can be suspended by Presidential order under Art.359, but Art.20 & 21 cannot.

⚠️ Trap Analysis

Trap: Students often think Art.32 (the very tool to enforce rights) cannot be suspended. But it CAN be suspended under Art.359 — only Art.20 and Art.21 cannot. The 44th Amendment (1978) enshrined this protection for Art.20 & 21 explicitly.

MCQ 02 — Conceptual
LOOKALIKE TERM

Which of the following is protected under Article 19(1)(a) (Freedom of Speech and Expression) as held by the Supreme Court?

A. Right to strike
B. Right to silence (not testifying)
C. Right to know (access to information)
D. Right to vote
✓ Correct Answer: C — Right to know / access to information

The SC in Secretary, Ministry of I&B v. Cricket Association of Bengal (1995) held that freedom of speech includes the right to receive information. The Right to Information Act (2005) flows from this.
Right to strike: Not a FR, only a statutory right under trade union law. Right to silence (not testifying): This is under Art.20(3) self-incrimination, not Art.19. Right to vote: A statutory right under Representation of People Act, not a FR.

⚠️ Trap Analysis

UPSC frequently tests what IS and ISN'T included under Art.19(1)(a). Right to strike, right to vote, right to remain silent — all sound like "freedoms" but are not under Art.19(1)(a). Right to information is the correct judicial expansion of speech and expression.

MCQ 03 — Article Matching
PARTIAL TRUTH

Which of the following Fundamental Rights are available to foreign nationals in India?
1. Art.14 (Equality before Law)
2. Art.15 (No discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex)
3. Art.20 (Protection from arbitrary conviction)
4. Art.21 (Right to Life)
Select the correct answer:

A. 1, 3 and 4 only
B. 1, 2, 3 and 4
C. 2, 3 and 4 only
D. 3 and 4 only
✓ Correct Answer: A — Art.14, 20, 21 are available to all persons including foreigners

The Constitution uses two words: "citizen" and "person". Rights using "citizen" are ONLY for citizens. Rights using "person" or with no qualifier are available to all.
• Art.14: "No person shall be denied..." — all persons ✓
• Art.15: "The State shall not discriminate... against any citizen" — citizens only ✗
• Art.20: "No person..." — all persons ✓
• Art.21: "No person..." — all persons ✓

⚠️ Trap Analysis

Classic UPSC statement-elimination. Students often assume Art.15 (against discrimination) is for all — but the text explicitly says "citizen." Always check the constitutional text. Citizens-only rights: Art.15, 16, 19, 29, 30. All-persons rights: Art.14, 20, 21, 22(1)(2), 23, 24, 25.

MCQ 04 — Doctrines
REVERSAL

Under which doctrine do pre-constitutional laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights become 'dormant' rather than 'dead' — and may revive if the relevant Fundamental Right is later amended?

A. Doctrine of Severability
B. Doctrine of Eclipse
C. Doctrine of Waiver
D. Doctrine of Colourable Legislation
✓ Correct Answer: B — Doctrine of Eclipse

The Doctrine of Eclipse (from Bhikaji Narain Dhakras v. State of MP, 1955) applies to pre-1950 laws. A law existing before the Constitution that conflicts with a FR becomes eclipsed (not void) — it is dormant for citizens but operative for non-citizens. If the FR is amended to permit such restriction, the law revives automatically. This is different from post-constitutional laws which are simply void under Art.13(2).

⚠️ Trap Analysis

Trap: Confusing Eclipse with Severability. Severability = only bad portion of a law is severed (applies to post-constitutional laws too). Eclipse = the whole law becomes dormant due to a FR shadow, but can revive. Remember: Eclipse → Sun (FR) eclipses the law; remove the eclipse (amend the FR) → law comes back to life.

MCQ 05 — Writ Jurisdiction
PARTIAL TRUTH

Which of the following statements about writs in India is/are correct?
1. The High Court can issue writs only for enforcement of Fundamental Rights.
2. A Writ of Mandamus can be issued against a private individual.
3. Habeas Corpus can be filed by any person on behalf of the detained person.

A. 1 and 2 only
B. 3 only
C. 1 and 3 only
D. 2 and 3 only
✓ Correct Answer: B — Only Statement 3 is correct

Statement 1 WRONG: HC under Art.226 can issue writs for enforcement of ANY legal right, not just FRs. SC under Art.32 is restricted to FR enforcement only.
Statement 2 WRONG: Mandamus is issued against public bodies/officials with a legal duty — not against private individuals (who have no public duty).
Statement 3 CORRECT: Habeas Corpus is a public writ — anyone (a friend, relative, or even a stranger) can file it on behalf of the wrongfully detained person. Even a letter to the SC can count as a petition (PIL era).

⚠️ Trap Analysis

Statement 1 is the classic confusion: "HC can only issue writs for FRs" — this is what SC does under Art.32. HC's Art.226 power is broader. Statement 2 traps students who remember "mandamus = you must act" and assume it applies to everyone. Remember: Mandamus = public DUTY = public BODY.

MCQ 06 — Amendment History
TRICK SEQUENCE

Match the Constitutional Amendment with what it did to Fundamental Rights:
1. 1st Amendment (1951) — Added Art.15(4): Special provisions for socially backward classes
2. 44th Amendment (1978) — Removed Right to Property from FR list
3. 86th Amendment (2002) — Inserted Art.21A: Right to Education
4. 97th Amendment (2011) — Added "cooperative societies" to Art.19(1)(c)
Which of the above is/are correct?

A. 1, 2 and 3 only
B. 2, 3 and 4 only
C. 1, 3 and 4 only
D. All four are correct
✓ Correct Answer: D — All four are correct

All four amendments are accurate:
• 1st (1951): Added Art.15(4) to overcome Champakam Dorairajan case which struck down reservations. Also added Art.19(2) restrictions (sovereignty etc.) and Art.31A, 31B (9th Schedule).
• 44th (1978): Removed Art.19(1)(f) and Art.31; added Art.300A for property.
• 86th (2002): Inserted Art.21A, amended Art.45 (DPSP) and Art.51A (duties) — the RTE framework.
• 97th (2011): Added "cooperative societies" to Art.19(1)(c). However, note that SC in 2021 partially struck down this amendment for encroaching on state subjects.

⚠️ Trap Analysis

UPSC tests sequence awareness. The trap is picking "all four correct" feels risky and students second-guess it. Verify each statement independently. Note: 97th Amendment's partial invalidation is a current affairs update — the amendment itself added cooperative societies to Art.19(1)(c), which is the factual claim being tested here.

MCQ 07 — Reservation Law
STATEMENT COMBO

With reference to reservations under Article 16, consider the following statements:
1. The 50% ceiling on reservations is a constitutionally mandated limit written in the text of Art.16.
2. The "creamy layer" concept applies to Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes as per the Supreme Court.
3. The 103rd Amendment (2019) allows up to 10% reservation for Economically Weaker Sections.
Which is/are correct?

A. 3 only
B. 1 and 3 only
C. 2 and 3 only
D. 1, 2 and 3
✓ Correct Answer: A — Only Statement 3 is correct

Statement 1 WRONG: The 50% cap is NOT in constitutional text — it was laid down by the SC in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) as a judge-made rule. It can be exceeded only in "extraordinary situations" (like Nagaland's 80% for tribal communities).
Statement 2 WRONG: The creamy layer concept applies to OBCs, NOT to SCs/STs. SC reaffirmed this in multiple cases including E.V. Chinnaiah and Jarnail Singh. In Jarnail Singh (2018), the SC said creamy layer cannot be applied to SCs/STs for post-promotion reservations.
Statement 3 CORRECT: The 103rd Amendment added Art.15(6) and 16(6), allowing EWS reservation up to 10%. Upheld by SC in Janhit Abhiyan (2022).

⚠️ Trap Analysis

Two very common misconceptions tested simultaneously: (1) thinking 50% is in the Constitution text (it's judicial), (2) thinking creamy layer applies to SCs/STs (it doesn't). UPSC uses this to filter students who know concepts shallowly vs deeply. Always check: Is this in the text of the Constitution, or is it a SC ruling?

MCQ 08 — Religion & State
ABSOLUTE WORD

Under Article 28 of the Constitution, which type of educational institution CANNOT provide religious instruction of any kind?

A. Institutions established under a trust providing religious instruction
B. Institutions established and wholly maintained by the State
C. Institutions recognised by the State
D. Institutions receiving aid from the State
✓ Correct Answer: B — Institutions established AND wholly maintained by the State

Art.28 has three tiers:
1. Wholly State-owned: No religious instruction at all (absolute prohibition).
2. State-aided (but set up under a trust/endowment requiring religious instruction): Can give religious instruction (because the religious character was the founding condition).
3. State-recognised or state-aided: Can give religious instruction, but no student can be compelled to attend.

⚠️ Trap Analysis

"State-recognised" and "State-aided" are often confused with "State-maintained." Only fully State-maintained institutions have the absolute bar. Aided schools can give optional religious instruction. This tests careful reading of Art.28 — the key phrase is "established and wholly maintained by the State."

MCQ 09 — Advanced Application
OUT OF SCOPE

Consider the following statements about Article 20 of the Constitution:
1. A person cannot be prosecuted and punished for the same offence more than once — this is the protection against Double Jeopardy.
2. Protection against self-incrimination under Art.20(3) extends to civil proceedings as well as criminal proceedings.
3. Art.20(1)'s ex-post facto protection means no person can be convicted under a law that did not exist when the act was committed.
Which of the above is/are correct?

A. 1 and 3 only
B. 1 only
C. 2 and 3 only
D. 1, 2 and 3
✓ Correct Answer: A — Statements 1 and 3 are correct

Statement 1 CORRECT: Art.20(2) — "No person shall be prosecuted and punished for the same offence more than once." This is Double Jeopardy protection.
Statement 2 WRONG: Art.20(3) self-incrimination protection applies ONLY to criminal proceedings — "No person accused of an offence shall be compelled to be a witness against himself." It does not extend to civil proceedings, where parties can be compelled to disclose documents.
Statement 3 CORRECT: Art.20(1) — Ex-post facto protection: no conviction under a law not in force when the act was committed; no penalty greater than that prescribed at the time.

⚠️ Trap Analysis

Statement 2 is a subtle "scope extension" trap — self-incrimination sounds like it should cover everything, but Art.20(3) uses the word "accused of an offence" — restricting it specifically to criminal cases. Also note: Double Jeopardy under Art.20(2) requires BOTH prosecution AND punishment in the first instance. If only prosecuted (acquitted), a fresh trial is possible in some circumstances — this is a deeper nuance UPSC may test.

MCQ 10 — UPSC Eliminator (Hardest)
STATEMENT COMBO

With reference to Fundamental Rights under the Indian Constitution, consider the following statements:
1. The Supreme Court can modify Fundamental Rights in their application to armed forces under Article 33 itself without Parliament making a law.
2. The President can suspend the right to move any court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights other than Art.20 and Art.21 during a National Emergency.
3. A Fundamental Right can be waived by a citizen voluntarily if it benefits them.
4. Fundamental Rights are available only against the State, never against private individuals.
Which of the above statements are CORRECT?

A. 2 only
B. 1 and 2 only
C. 2 and 4 only
D. 1, 3 and 4 only
✓ Correct Answer: A — Only Statement 2 is correct

Statement 1 WRONG: Art.33 says Parliament can make a law restricting/modifying FR application to armed forces. The SC cannot do this on its own — it requires a Parliamentary law.
Statement 2 CORRECT: This is precisely what Art.359 allows — Presidential proclamation can suspend enforcement of any FRs (except Art.20 and 21) during a National Emergency.
Statement 3 WRONG: FRs CANNOT be waived — this is the Doctrine of Non-Waiver. The SC held in Behram Khurshid Pesikaka v. State of Bombay (1955) that FRs are conferred on citizens not for individual benefit alone but as a matter of public policy.
Statement 4 WRONG: While FRs primarily operate against the State, Art.17 (untouchability) and Art.23 (forced labour) operate against private individuals too — these have horizontal effect.

⚠️ Trap Analysis

This is a classic UPSC eliminator — each statement contains a plausible-sounding falsehood. Statement 1 is tempting because "SC modifies rights" sounds right. Statement 3 feels intuitive — "if it benefits you, you should be able to waive it." Statement 4 is the most commonly believed half-truth. The only way through: know each statement's nuance cold. UPSC rewards depth over breadth.

📰 Current Affairs (2024–2026)

[P+M] February 2024

Electoral Bonds Case — Art.19(1)(a) Landmark

Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (Feb 15, 2024) — A 5-judge Constitution Bench unanimously struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme (2018).

  • Violation: The scheme allowed anonymous donations to political parties, preventing voters from knowing who funds parties. The SC held this violated Art.19(1)(a) — freedom of information as part of freedom of speech and expression.
  • Proportionality Test: Court applied the proportionality doctrine — even if the goal (donor privacy) was legitimate, the means (total anonymity) failed the proportionality standard.
  • The State Bank of India was directed to disclose all bond details to the Election Commission of India, which was to publish them.
Mains angle: Discusses the tension between Art.19(1)(a) (right to information), Art.19(1)(g) (freedom of trade — donors' right), and the integrity of electoral democracy. Key doctrines: Proportionality, Reasonable Restriction, Basic Structure (free and fair elections).

Electoral Bonds → Art.19(1)(a) Violation → SC Ruling 2024

[P+M] 2025

Right to Digital Access — Art.21 Extended

The Supreme Court held in 2025 that access to digital services is a Fundamental Right under Article 21, particularly for persons with disabilities. The judgment arose from a petition challenging inaccessible KYC verification systems.

  • The Court directed the government to ensure inclusive KYC methods, screen reader compatibility, and accessible digital interfaces.
  • This continues the Art.21 expansion trend — joining the right to food, health, education, and privacy.
  • Connects with the Digital India initiative and the government's obligation to not create exclusionary digital infrastructure.
Prelims fact: Right to internet access as FR was first explored in Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India (2020) — SC held that freedom of press under Art.19(1)(a) includes digital media, and internet shutdowns must satisfy proportionality.
[P] 2025

Narco-Test Ruling — Art.20(3) Reaffirmed

The SC reiterated in 2025 that narco-analysis tests and lie-detector tests cannot be administered without the consent of the individual, reaffirming Art.20(3) (protection against self-incrimination).

  • This builds on Selvi v. State of Karnataka (2010) where a 3-judge bench held that involuntary narco-tests violate Art.20(3) and Art.21.
  • Prelims fact: Art.20(3) protects against being a "witness against oneself" — applies only in criminal proceedings, only to accused persons, and covers testimonial compulsion (not physical evidence like fingerprints).
[P+M] December 2023

Art.370 Abrogation Upheld — FRs in J&K

In In Re: Article 370 (Dec 11, 2023), a 5-judge SC Constitution Bench unanimously upheld the abrogation of Art.370 in 2019.

  • Post-abrogation, all Fundamental Rights in Part III now apply to Jammu & Kashmir without any modifications.
  • Key FR angle: Prior to 2019, J&K had a separate Constitution that restricted some FRs (e.g., property rights, citizenship). Art.35A (derived from Art.370) prevented people from outside J&K from acquiring property — this was annulled.
  • The SC held that Article 370 was a temporary provision and the President had the power to abrogate it.
Mains angle: Discuss the tension between federalism, state autonomy, and the uniform application of FRs. The judgment reaffirms that FRs are the bedrock of the constitutional framework — even special provisions like Art.370 cannot permanently derogate from them.
[M] 2022 (ongoing relevance)

EWS Reservation — 103rd Amendment Upheld

In Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (Nov 2022), a 5-judge SC bench by 3:2 upheld the 103rd Constitutional Amendment which introduced 10% reservation for Economically Weaker Sections (EWS) under Art.15(6) and Art.16(6).

  • Key holding: EWS reservation does not violate the basic structure or equality code — it promotes substantive equality by addressing economic disadvantage.
  • Minority opinion (Justices Ravindra Bhat & Hima Kohli): The exclusion of SCs/STs/OBCs from EWS is discriminatory and violates the equality code.
  • The 50% cap established in Indra Sawhney was clarified: the EWS 10% is over and above the existing reservations, not included within the 50%.
Prelims fact: EWS reservation criteria: Annual family income below ₹8 lakh + agricultural land less than 5 acres + residential plot less than 100 sq yards in notified municipality.
